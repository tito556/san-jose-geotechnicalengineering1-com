Approach and scope

We see the same mistake repeated across San Jose: a geotech report with three borings and a generic Site Class D assumption, applied to a five-acre mixed-use parcel that actually straddles two distinct soil columns. The structural engineer runs the ETABS model, the contractor prices the rebar, and six months later a peer reviewer flags the missing site response analysis. Now the permit is on hold, and the owner is paying for an acceleration spectrum that should have been part of the initial scope. Seismic microzonation prevents that gap. Our approach layers cone penetration data with geophysical profiles to map the impedance contrast at the sediment-bedrock interface, then runs 1D equivalent-linear site response using DEEPSOIL or Strata. We deliver maps of peak ground acceleration, spectral acceleration at 0.2s and 1.0s, and amplification factors that align with ASCE 7-22 Chapter 21. In San Jose's Berryessa district, where the Hayward Fault trace runs beneath residential neighborhoods, Alquist-Priolo zone setbacks are mandatory, but the shaking hazard extends far beyond the rupture plane—and microzonation captures that attenuation pattern. We also verify Vs profiles through cross-hole testing when the site geometry allows, ensuring the inversion models aren't just mathematically plausible but physically measured. The output integrates directly with the geotechnical baseline report, so the structural team isn't guessing at the site coefficients.

Site-specific factors

ASCE 7-22 Section 11.4.8 requires site-specific ground motion analysis for structures on Site Class D with S1 ≥ 0.2, and in San Jose, large portions of the valley floor exceed that trigger. Skipping microzonation when it's code-mandated isn't just a technical shortcut—it's a liability that can surface during plan check or, worse, after an event. The California Geological Survey's Seismic Hazard Zone maps identify liquefaction and landslide potential, but they operate at 1:24,000 scale; they weren't designed to dictate foundation parameters for a five-story podium slab. A microzonation study fills that regulatory gap with defensible numbers. We've seen projects along Monterey Road where the generalized map said one thing, but the CPT soundings and Vs profile told a different story—one that changed the foundation system from shallow footings to ground improvement. Deep soft clay in the Evergreen basin amplifies long-period energy, affecting mid-rise structures exactly where the standard code spectrum under-predicts demand. When the city's building official asks for a peer-reviewed site response, having a microzonation report backed by measured Vs data and IBC-compliant procedures keeps the permit moving. For critical facilities, this also feeds into the liquefaction assessment and the seismic soil-structure interaction model.

Common questions

What does a seismic microzonation study cost in San Jose?

For a typical parcel-scale study in San Jose, costs range from US$3,850 for a single-profile site response to US$17,510 for a multi-profile microzonation with GIS deliverables across a larger site. The spread depends on the number of Vs measurement locations, whether we use MASW or downhole methods, and the complexity of the site response modeling. A site with deep soft clay requiring nonlinear analysis will land on the higher end. We carry out a fixed-fee scope after reviewing the geotechnical baseline and the structural engineer's requirements.

How is microzonation different from the state seismic hazard maps?

The California Geological Survey maps identify zones where liquefaction or earthquake-induced landslides could occur, at a regional scale. Microzonation goes much finer: it delivers parcel-level or neighborhood-level maps of ground shaking intensity—PGA, spectral acceleration, and amplification—based on measured soil dynamics, not just geology. It's the difference between knowing you're in a Zone of Required Investigation and having a site coefficient that goes directly into the lateral load calculations.

When does San Jose's building code require a site-specific study?

Under CBC and ASCE 7-22, a site-specific ground motion analysis is required when the structure is assigned to Seismic Design Category D, E, or F and the site class is D through F, with S1 exceeding 0.2g. Many San Jose valley locations trigger this, especially where soft clay exceeds 30 feet in thickness. The building official may also require it for essential facilities, tall buildings over 240 feet, or structures on potentially liquefiable soils where standard code spectra aren't reliable.
